  Andrew Carnegie is known for his successful career as an entrepreneur and industrialist, as well as his philanthropic efforts. He was a self-educated "working boy" who emigrated from Scotland and rose to prominence in Pittsburgh, Pennsylvania, where he founded what became the world's largest steel producing company. Carnegie was also known for his belief that "to die rich is to die disgraced," and he donated $1 million to establish a technical institute in Pittsburgh, which later became Carnegie Mellon University. Today, Carnegie Mellon University is a global leader in higher education and research, known for its innovation, interdisciplinary collaboration, and commitment to creating opportunities for the general public.
  The arrival of the first IBM computer at Carnegie Tech in 1956 revolutionized the university culture by introducing a new era of information technology that permeated various academic disciplines. The computer's magnetic-drum memory and processing speed of approximately 60 instructions per second enabled the university to conduct more complex research and analysis, expanding its capabilities in fields such as engineering, science, and business. This development marked the beginning of a new era of technological advancement and innovation at Carnegie Tech, which continued to grow and evolve throughout the latter half of the 20th century.
